# Career Strategy Reference

Long-term career planning frameworks, cross-industry mobility data, and coaching methodology backed by RCT evidence. Load this file when users need career pivot strategy, long-term planning, or structured coaching.

## Required Inputs

Before advising on career strategy, collect:
- Current role, industry, and tenure
- Target direction (specific role, industry change, seniority jump, or "I don't know")
- What's driving the change (dissatisfaction, layoff, growth ceiling, curiosity)
- Financial constraints (can they take a pay cut for a transition? How long?)
- Skills they have vs skills they think they need

## Career Coaching Effectiveness (T1 — RCT Evidence)

This is peer-reviewed, randomized controlled trial data. Coaching works.

| Group | Employment rate (2nd year) | Monthly earnings | Tier |
|-------|---------------------------|-----------------|------|
| Career coaching | 95% | $2,568 | T1 |
| Standard support (control) | 83% | $1,865 | T1 |
| **Delta** | **+12 percentage points** | **+$703/month** | T1 |

Additional findings:
- Coach assignment was the single best predictor of job acquisition [T1]
- Frequency of meetings and trust quality were primary success drivers [T1]
- Vocational training alone: 70-80% placement. Coaching: 95%. Coaching outperforms training by ~15 points [T1]
- Meta-analysis: job satisfaction has significant positive correlation with job performance [T1]

**What this means for the skill**: The interviewer mode isn't a nice-to-have — it implements a proven intervention. Structured coaching that builds trust and meets regularly produces measurably better outcomes than resume templates alone.

## Coaching Frameworks

Three validated frameworks for structuring career conversations.

| Model | Phases | Tier |
|-------|--------|------|
| GROW | Goal → Reality → Options → Will | T1 |
| OSCAR | Outcome → Situation → Choices → Actions → Review | T1 |
| CLEAR | Contract → Listening → Exploring → Action → Review | T1 |

### Which to Use When

- **GROW**: Best for goal-oriented sessions where the user knows what they want but not how to get there
- **OSCAR**: Best for complex situations with multiple possible paths — the "Choices" phase forces explicit comparison
- **CLEAR**: Best for ongoing relationships where contracting (setting expectations) matters

### Optimal Engagement

4-12 sessions produce the best outcomes [T2]. The skill should aim to create value in every session while building toward a longer arc.

### Session Arc [T2]

| Sessions | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| 1 | Contracting and goal-setting |
| 2-3 | Values exploration, resource mapping |
| 4-6 | Accomplishment mining, skill reframing |
| 7-10 | Execution: resume building, mock interviews, applications |
| 11-12 | Review, adjustment, exit planning |

In a single-session context (which is most common for this skill), compress: Phase 1 intake → Phase 3 mining → immediate execution with the deliverable they came for.

## Cross-Industry Mobility

Not all industries are equally permeable. This data determines how realistic a pivot is and how to approach it.

### Open vs Closed Circuits

| Sector | Entry from outside field | Circuit type | Tier |
|--------|------------------------|--------------|------|
| Hospitality | 88% | Open | T2 |
| Retail | 74% | Open | T2 |
| Logistics | 69% | Open | T2 |
| Nursing | Only 34% from outside | Closed | T2 |
| Software Development | Low (specific % absent) | Closed | T2 |

### The Financial Reality

Cross-industry moves carry a financial penalty: lower initial compensation compared to within-industry moves [T1, peer-reviewed executive compensation research].

This penalty is temporary but real. Users considering a pivot need to:
1. Know it exists (don't sugarcoat it)
2. Plan financially for 6-12 months of potentially lower earnings
3. Understand it as an investment with a recovery timeline, not a permanent downgrade

### Pivoting Into Closed-Circuit Industries

When the target industry has low outside entry (nursing, software, etc.):
- **Credential gap**: Identify minimum required certifications or training
- **Bridge roles**: Find roles that straddle both industries (healthcare admin for healthcare, tech support for software)
- **Demonstrate don't declare**: Build a portfolio project or complete a relevant certification before claiming readiness

## Industry Translation

How to reframe experience from one industry for another.

### Terminology Mapping [T3]

Reframe prior activities in the target industry's language:

| What you did | How the source industry says it | How the target industry hears it |
|-------------|--------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Organized a community event | Event coordination | Project management, stakeholder engagement |
| Managed a restaurant kitchen | Kitchen management | Operations management, team leadership, P&L ownership |
| Taught classes | Teaching, instruction | Training, enablement, curriculum development |
| Ran a retail store | Store management | Revenue ownership, inventory optimization, team development |

The skill should propose translations to the user for confirmation before using them.

### The 30-Day Skill Sprint [T3]

A structured approach to demonstrating cross-industry value:

**Week 1**: Identify market signals — what problems does the target industry have that your skills could solve?

**Week 2**: Pick one problem. Automate or prototype a solution using tools you already know (spreadsheets, AI tools, no-code platforms).

**Week 3**: Package the result as a "Playbook" or "Training Deck" — a tangible artifact that demonstrates domain understanding.

**Week 4**: Share it. Post on LinkedIn (see linkedin.md content strategy), send to contacts in the target industry (see outreach.md), use it in applications.

The sprint produces a concrete deliverable that proves cross-industry value better than any resume claim.

## Long-Term Career Frameworks

Frameworks for thinking beyond the next job.

### Tim Ferriss — Lifestyle Design
Define the life you want first, then reverse-engineer the career that supports it. The "ideal Tuesday" question from the interviewer mode comes from this approach.

### Cal Newport — Career Capital Theory
Skills are currency. Build rare and valuable skills, then trade them for the work life you want. Don't follow passion — build skills that create opportunities.

### Michael Dalton — Deliberate Career Progression
Map career moves as a sequence: each role should build a specific capability that the next role requires. Random moves accumulate experience but not leverage.

### Ramit Sethi — Wealth Strategy
Negotiate hard on the big things (salary, career moves), don't optimize the small things (daily coffee). Career decisions have 10x more financial impact than frugality.

### Bill Burnett / Dave Evans — Designing Your Life
Prototype career options before committing. Informational interviews, job shadowing, and short-term projects test hypotheses without risk.

## Accomplishment Mining for Career Strategy

The same techniques from the interviewer mode, applied to long-term planning.

### Recognition Sequence [T2]
"What skills do others recognize and appreciate in you?"

### Flow Sequence [T2]
"What activities make you lose track of time? What does that reveal about where you should be heading?"

### Counterfactual [T2]
"If your team lost you tomorrow, what would break first?" — reveals your unique value, which should inform your career direction.

### RISE Method [T2]
- **Record**: 15 minutes every Friday noting wins
- **Include** impact: what changed because of what you did
- **Capture** skills: what the win demonstrated
- **Embrace** your story: stop minimizing

Particularly effective for imposter syndrome — separates professional worth from personal value.
